Chapter 1

Passive Indexing

Passive indexing is the practice of tracking a broad benchmark (for example, a total-market or S&P 500 index) using rules-based allocation, low turnover, and cost discipline.

Why It Matters in Investing

Passive indexing matters because it reframes investing as an exposure problem rather than a prediction problem. Instead of guessing which single asset will outperform next, investors can capture broad market growth while minimizing fees, taxes, and behavioral errors.

For quantitative finance learners, this chapter is foundational: it introduces benchmark definitions, portfolio weighting logic, rebalancing mechanics, and evaluation metrics that recur in more advanced strategy design.

Explanation

A passive index portfolio usually follows a transparent rule: choose a benchmark universe, assign weights (market-cap or alternative), rebalance on a fixed schedule, and measure tracking error against the target index.

Even this simple process introduces core quant concepts:

  • Data hygiene: handling missing prices and corporate actions.
  • Portfolio construction: weight normalization and constraints.
  • Performance analytics: return attribution, drawdowns, and turnover.
